IPCN State Symbols

The Official Seal & Flag of the Sovereign Nation.

Adopted in 2024, these official symbols represent the unity, ecological heritage, continuous custom, and future trajectory of the Indigenous Peoples of Cordillera Nation.

Official Emblem

The Circular Seal of IPCN

The circular seal represents the completeness of the ancestral domain. The green geometric border displays traditional Cordilleran weave patterns representing culture. The mountain valley and suspension footbridge highlight the natural highland environment we defend. The two hands in the center represent the creation of autonomy and peaceful unity among the tribes. The seven stars along the bottom symbolize the six provinces of the Cordillera and the Contiguous Areas established since 2024, with the final label cleanly integrated in gold and black in the bottom center.

National Banner

The Official IPCN Flag

The IPCN flag features a warm yellow-cream background symbolizing peace and rich agricultural land. The central blue octagon represents structural stability and clarity of governance. Inside, the glowing comet/shooting star with a dust trail symbolizes a guided path toward future progress and enlightenment. The octagon is surrounded by seven red stars representing the collective strengths and sovereignty of the six highland provinces plus the Contiguous Area.

Customary Self-Governance

Ancestral Sovereignty

An ancient domain older than the state that surrounds it.

Long before lowland colonial systems mapped the highlands, the communities of the Cordillera Central range sustained their lands through strict customary borders, peace treaties, and collective councils.

Pre-colonial continuity in the modern era

The Cordillera mountain spine is not just a geographic structure, but an active socio-political space. Unconquered through three centuries of Spanish colonization, our institutions represent an unbroken connection to ancestral land governance.

IPCN serves as a modern governance interface, protecting the collective territorial rights of Cordillera nations. We verify boundary demarcations, implement communal land management policies, and coordinate local development efforts across all seven provinces and contiguous areas.

By registering domains and securing Free, Prior and Informed Consent (FPIC), we establish a modern operational framework that respects our elders' councils while enforcing constitutional rights under the Indigenous Peoples' Rights Act.

Peoples & Culture

Kaigorotan — many peoples, one range.

We are distinct societies with unique languages, weaving traditions, and customary laws, united by our shared geography, kinship, and historical defense of our domain.

Tinggian (Itneg)

Abra

Homeland in Abra. Renowned for their intricate binakol and pinilian geometric weaving, sugarcane wine (basi) rites, and the community lapat forest conservation system.

Ibaloy

Benguet

Agrarian leaders of southern Benguet. Known for gold-working heritage, cattle raising, and intricate oral epics describing centuries of terrace farming.

Kankanaey

Benguet · Mt. Province

Builders of the dap-ay council systems. Famous for advanced backstrap loom weaving and deep expertise in highland terrace construction.

Bontoc

Mountain Province

Governors of Chico River villages. Organized through ator houses and historically renowned for resisting aggressive mining and logging exploitation.

Ifugao

Ifugao

Architects of the UNESCO rice terrace systems. Pioneered the muyong agro-forestry network, widely cited as a model for modern ecological preservation.

Kalinga

Kalinga

Keepers of the bodong peace treaty protocol. Turned age-old diplomatic structures into region-wide alliances to protect mountain corridors.

Isneg (Apayao)

Apayao

Inhabitants of the Apayao-Abulog river corridors. Practicing swidden agriculture and riverine fishing in houses styled after boat shapes.

Self-Governance

Traditional systems meeting legal frameworks.

Self-rule is defined by functional institutions. We bridge customary law with modern national policies to establish secure autonomy.

Dap-ay / Ator Assembly

Municipal Councils

Local community houses where elders resolve property disputes, plan agricultural cycles, and enforce customary justice. They form the foundational layer of IPCN's collective assembly.

Bodong Peace Pacts

Inter-tribal Treaties

Bilateral treaties between tribal regions that regulate borders, resolve conflicts, and permit trade routes. This system acts as a decentralized diplomatic network across the mountain range.

Muyong, Lapat & Batangan

Ecological Stewardship

Traditional community-enforced forest preservation zones. Logging and harvesting are strictly regulated to preserve local water tables and prevent watershed soil erosion.

Custom meets Constitutional Law

The Indigenous Peoples' Rights Act of 1997 (Republic Act No. 8371) provides the legal recognition for customary systems within the Philippine Republic:

  • CADT Titles: Legal recognition of ancestral domain borders verified by geodetic surveyors.
  • FPIC Consent: Veto power over external commercial infrastructure or resource extraction.
  • Customary Justice: Local dispute resolution by elders recognized in courts.
  • Administrative Integration: Linking traditional councils directly to municipal and provincial local government units.

History & Resistance

A legacy of defending our ancestral highlands.

Our autonomy was not gifted; it was fought for and defended. The IPCN documents this historical path of preservation and resistance.

1500s–1800s

Three centuries of highland independence

While Spanish colonial forces governed the lowlands, the Cordillera mountain communities maintained independence through defensive combat and organized council networks.

1970s

The Chico River and Cellophil resistance

Highland communities organized to block the Chico River Hydroelectric Dam and Marcos-era timber concessions in Abra (Cellophil Resources), protecting ancestral lands and sacred burial sites.

Apr 24, 1980

Martyrdom of Macli-ing Dulag

Kalinga Pangat (chieftain) Macli-ing Dulag was assassinated for leading the opposition against the Chico Dam, unifying the entire region and stopping the dam's implementation.

1987

Creation of the interim CAR

Executive Order 220 established the Cordillera Administrative Region as an interim structure to prepare the area for full regional autonomy.

1997

Passage of IPRA (RA 8371)

The landmark Indigenous Peoples' Rights Act recognized ancestral domain land titles, giving customary self-governance a formal legal standing.

Present Day

Modernization and Autonomy

From the secretariat in Peñarrubia, IPCN drives education, smart mapping alignment, and local council coordination, working toward a fully self-reliant Cordillera.
